    Abstract

    For this project, our group attempted to study a financial statistic called the Personal Savings Rate. This measure is collected by the US government and is defined as the ratio of money saved by individuals or families to their disposable income (income after taxes). This figure has been in decline overall since 1975, when Americans saved 17% of their disposable income; today Americans save only 2.8% (as of April 2018). Various scholarly research studies have attempted to tie this metric to a variety of factors, among them: inflation, recessions, real wages, stock market investment, ease of consumer credit, cultural attitudes about savings, as well as individual spending categories such as healthcare, housing prices, and the cost of higher education. There is not consensus among economists about what causes this rate to fluctuate, nor do they agree on whether a low rate is “good” or “bad” for an economy since it could mean that Americans have less money to save or that wealthier Americans have so much confidence in the economy that they have pulled assets from traditional savings accounts in order to invest them.
